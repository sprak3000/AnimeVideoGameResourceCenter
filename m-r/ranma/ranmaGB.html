Ranma 1/2

  • Genre: Puzzle game
    Controls
  • A button: Push Object
  • B button: Push Object
  • Start: Pause
  • Select: [not used]
Despite the poor graphics, this game is very entertaining. You must retrieve three items (a ribbon, a hat, and some gloves) to have Akane appear. Once Akane appears, you must tag her to complete the level. In order to get these items, you must push around boxes, rocks, and logs to get to them. Boxes and rocks can be broken, but to remove logs, you must push two or more of them togther. Of course, Ranma's rivals and girlfriends are chasing him around trying to stop him.

The puzzles are quite challenging at times. Boxes can be broken open to reveal hot or cold water. These can change you back and forth from male to female Ranma. Male Ranma causes objects to move until they hit something else. Female Ranma can only move objects one spot at a time. You must figure out when each form is appropriate. With 50 different puzzles to solve, you won't lose interest in this one for awhile. Besides the poor graphics, my only other complaint is that there is no password or saving options. You have three continues at your disposal, but no way to stop the game and come back to it later.

Ranma 1/2: Kakugeki Mondou!

  • Genre: Strategy/Puzzle game

From Roger Nystrom:

As best as I can translate, the title means Status Play Dialogue. It is a strategy game of an undescribable type. You play Ranma in the story mode, who goes around different parts of Japan and meets two types of people; the first are ones that have various reasons for beating the living snot out of Ranma (for example, in the first stage, there's the Principal, Kodachi, Kunou, Gosunkugi, and Ryouga), and characters which will ask you questions for points.

When you encounter battle characters, they will have a small dialogue and then you will be at a screen showing both your picture and your opponent's pictures, special move meters, and life meters. Pressing A button will start a slot-machine-esque box (Lucky Panel) up, and pressing A button again stops it. This decides your luck in the match. Sunny is the best, an umbrella means iffy, and a lemon means you're the farthest from having any luck of the sort. Next will pop up your various battle attributes - the two top are Power and Special Moves, and the bottom two are Defense and Life. Pressing B button and A button will lower and raise the various attributes, depending on how many points you have. If you are satisfied with the adjustments, move to the first bottom Japanese word and hit A button; choosing the other one will reset the attributes. A menu will then show up, letting you select various moves, which are abbreviated with DF (defend), AT (attack), and SAT (Super Attack). Super attacks are the strongest but take off a ton off your meter.

Attacks are potent, but will do nothing if countered with a super attack. The battle will begin when you press A button to move to the selections (Fight or Reset); your first move will be paired up with your opponent's first; your second with his/her second, and so on until either all moves are done, or until someone runs out of life. The reward for winning is more points for your battle attributes.

The question characters (Akane, Shampoo, and the like) will ask questions that pertain to Ranma knowledge; this is where even a little Japanese knowledge helps, as does an emulator with a pause feature. Typical questions range from "Which word describes Akane's cooking" (Warui - bad) to "Which character first kissed Ranma?" (Mikado) The object is to answer six questions correctly before time runs out; each time you're wrong you are penalized with more time subtracted. The reward is again, more points.

It helps in this game to scout out the town, remembering which houses have what. Go for all of the question charaters first; even if your Japanese knowledge is nonexistent, try going for Kasumi or Shampoo in the first stage for example, where you'll have a 50/50 shot of being right, and your continues are unlimited, so you can go at it as long as it takes. In the first stage go for Gosunkugi and the Principal, and be sure to have your power boosted up high (A hundred or more is excellent!) All characters except Kodachi and very few others always seem to block on the third move; use this to your advantage.

Also, even if you only have one Game Boy or one emulator, the third option on the main menu (Versus) works for two players, each taking turns planning out his/her character of choice (even Tsubasa is in this one!), luck, attributes, and moves. While this isn't really a game that requires skill, it can test how well you can predict and outwit your opponent.
